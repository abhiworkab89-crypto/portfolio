RTA Dubai - Traffic Fine Payment Redesign

Redesigning the traffic fine payment experience for the Roads & Transport Authority app clearer, faster, and more trustworthy for residents in Dubai.

UX / UI Designer
Android / iOS
Figma
2026

Overview

The RTA app is a government service used by Dubai residents for transport tasks like paying traffic fines, renewing vehicle registration, and accessing public transport. This project focused on redesigning the traffic fine payment experience to make it clearer, faster, and less stressful to use.

Most users open the app knowing exactly what they want to do, but the existing flow made that harder than it needed to be. The redesign covered the full payment journey across 8 screens, from Home all the way to Receipt.

Problem & Pain Points

The core issue was that the app wasn't built around how people actually use it. The home screen felt like a directory rather than a task tool too many services competing for attention, no clear categories, and no obvious path to the thing you came to do.

Payment screens were just as rough. Fine amounts and breakdowns were inconsistently laid out, CTAs were hard to read at a glance, and there was no proper confirmation or receipt at the end. Users were left wondering if their payment actually went through.

Design Decisions

The Home screen was redesigned as a task launcher, not a content hub. Quick Actions and Action Required items are front and center so users can move straight to what they came for. All services are grouped into six categories under a Services tab, which keeps Home clean and makes the app scalable without adding clutter.

Once users enter the payment flow, the bottom navigation disappears. The reasoning is simple: payment is a linear task and distractions during that moment cost trust. A dedicated Fine Details screen shows the full amount breakdown before anything is confirmed, and a Confirm Payment step with clear checkboxes means users always know what they're agreeing to.

My Contribution

I started with a UX audit of the existing app and looked at how other government payment flows are structured. From there I defined the user persona and mapped a flow covering 8 screens: Home, Services, Traffic Fines List, Fine Details, Confirm Payment, Payment Method, Payment Success, and Receipt.

Wireframes came first to lock down layout and hierarchy, then I moved into high-fidelity in Figma using Dubai Font and a blue palette aligned to government standards. I also built a component library with reusable cards, input states, button variants, and status indicators to keep everything consistent across the flow.

Output & Learnings

The project delivered 8 complete high-fidelity screens, a Figma component library, and a visual system aligned to Dubai government standards.

The thing that stuck with me: in apps where people are dealing with money or compliance, trust is built in the details. Hiding the nav during payment, showing a proper receipt, breaking down the fine amount clearly none of these are flashy decisions, but each one reduces anxiety and makes the experience feel like it was built for the person using it. Good design in this space is mostly about knowing what to take away.

YONO SBI - Banking App Redesign

Redesigning India's most-used banking super app - making everyday financial tasks faster, clearer, and more trustworthy for millions of SBI customers.

UX / UI Designer
Banking
Figma
2025

Overview

YONO is SBI's unified digital banking platform designed to let users manage all their financial needs in one place - from checking balances and making transfers to investments and bill payments. Despite its scale, the existing app struggled with navigation complexity, slow performance, and a cluttered interface that made even simple tasks feel difficult.

This project focused on redesigning the core user experience, improving login, the home screen, service discovery, and the money transfer flow to make everyday banking faster, smoother, and more secure for millions of SBI customers.

Problem & Pain Points

After reviewing user feedback and app store reviews, three major problem areas emerged. 79% of users reported difficulty navigating, struggling to find basic features due to a cluttered interface and unclear menu structure. 67% experienced slow app performance, with lag during transactions and occasional app freezes. And 54% reported login and OTP issues - delays, auto-logouts, and failed logins that created frustration and distrust.

Users described it clearly: "Simple things like checking my balance take too many steps." "The OTP doesn't arrive." "The app crashes when I open account details - it makes me feel insecure about using it for important transactions." These were not minor inconveniences - they were actively eroding trust in a product people rely on for their finances.

Design Decisions

The login flow was redesigned with faster OTP delivery, biometric authentication support, and a stable session system - ensuring users can access their accounts quickly and safely every time. The Home screen was reorganized as an action-first dashboard: a clear account overview, quick-action shortcuts for Pay, Credit Card, Mutual Funds, and Bills, and a UPI QR code for instant payments - all visible without scrolling.

Services were grouped into clear categories - Recharge & Bills, Travel & Booking, and Financial Products, with an expandable FAQ section and direct SBI support at the bottom. The money transfer flow was simplified to four clear steps: Choose Bank Transfer, Enter Receiver Details, Enter Amount, and Review & Confirm. After completing a transfer, users receive a structured digital receipt with transaction ID, date, amount, and options to download or share.

My Contribution

I began by reviewing user feedback to identify the three primary problem areas, then created a user persona representing the majority of task-based, time-sensitive users. I mapped a complete user flow - from app open through Splash, Login (Phone to OTP), Home, Service Discovery, Money Transfer, and Payment Confirmation through to Receipt.

Low-fidelity wireframes were built first to establish layout and flow, before moving into high-fidelity design. All screens were designed in Figma using a unified design system - Roboto typography, a magenta and dark palette aligned to YONO's brand identity, and a reusable icon library. I also created 3D visuals for the Services section to enhance the presentation and bring the redesign to life.

Output & Learnings

This project delivered complete high-fidelity screens for the full banking journey, a Figma component library, a visual system aligned to SBI's brand, and 3D visuals for the services section.

The biggest learning: in banking, trust is designed, not assumed. Every detail - the confirmation step before a transfer, the structured receipt, and biometric login signals to the user that the app is safe and reliable. Without these signals, users abandon transactions. Good product design in financial services is inseparable from building confidence at every single step.

Product Designer at Editage - Cactus Communication

1 year 4 months designing digital experiences for a global academic research platform across web design, UI systems, illustration, banner design, and multiple regional markets.

Cactus Communication
Editage
1 Year 4 Months
India Japan Korea

What I Was Doing

Editage is one of the world's largest academic editing and research services platforms, part of Cactus Communication. I joined as a Product Designer in a team of 1012 members, working on user-facing digital products across multiple product verticals and regional markets. My work spanned web page design, landing page design, UI component work, illustration, icon design, and marketing banner creation with projects reaching audiences in India, Japan, and Korea.

My focus was on making complex research services feel simple, accessible, and trustworthy for academics and researchers around the world balancing brand consistency with regional expectations across three distinct markets.

Projects I Worked On

During my time at Editage, I contributed to five key projects each requiring a different design approach, from full product UI to localised marketing campaigns.

About Us Page (Editage). The About Us page needed to be redesigned in line with the OneResearcher launch. I started by researching how competitor websites structure their About Us pages, then created wireframes and planned the content flow. The final design clearly conveys Editage's vision and services as a unified ecosystem, kept minimal and in line with the existing design system.

Translation Service for Pharmaceutical & Life Sciences. For a section titled "We speak the language of science and of your audience," I designed a visual system with three key content offerings: Videos, Infographics, and Social Media. Using a 12-column grid, I created illustrations and a clean, minimal icon set to represent each content type, ensuring users could instantly understand the purpose of each option.

Grant Landing Page (Korea). This project involved creating a landing page for the 2nd 2025 Editage Scholarship for Emerging Researchers. The challenge was to communicate program details, selection process, eligibility, judges, and important dates in a way that was easy to follow, visually engaging, and trustworthy for potential applicants. I structured the page with clear sections and used visual hierarchy and icons to guide users through the content.

Multi Journal Formatting Banner (Korea). I designed a clear, compelling banner promoting unlimited journal formatting for a year, targeting frequent submitters in the Korean market. The design focused on the main message with a bold headline, an illustration, a highlighted price, and a strong call-to-action keeping the layout simple and easy to understand at a glance.

Multi Journal Formatting Hero Banners (Japan). I designed multiple hero banner sections for the Japanese Editage website across different campaign types from brand awareness banners to promotional campaigns for formatting services and scholarship programmes. Each banner was designed to match the brand style, user needs, and messaging direction from stakeholders.

Skills I Applied

Across these five projects I applied user research and competitor analysis, wireframing and content structuring, high-fidelity UI design, illustration and custom icon creation, visual design and typography, localisation for Japanese and Korean markets, and component-based design system thinking to maintain consistency across a complex product ecosystem.

Overall

Working at Cactus Communication gave me the experience of designing for a real, global product used by millions of researchers. I learned to balance business goals with user needs, work across multiple markets with different cultural expectations, and ship designs that are both visually strong and functionally clear.

It reinforced that good design in a complex domain requires simplicity above everything else. Whether it's a landing page for a pharmaceutical translation client or a hero banner campaign for Korean academics, the job is always the same make it easier to understand, easier to act on, and easier to trust.

WorkGrid - Workforce Management Platform

Designing a workforce management platform for mid-scale IT enterprises from first sketch to live production a full-stack SaaS product designed and shipped solo.

Product Designer
Web SaaS Desktop
2 week
Live in production

Overview

WorkGrid is a workforce management platform built for mid-scale IT enterprises companies with 500 to 5,000 employees who need more than a basic HRMS, but can't justify the cost or complexity of SAP or Workday. This is an original product built for portfolio purposes, not a redesign. I handled everything from research and architecture to UI design and production build, co-developed with Claude AI as an engineering partner.

The product ships as 7 interconnected modules sharing a single design system and data model: Dashboard, Employees, Projects, Allocation, Leave, Timesheets, and Reports.

Problem & Pain Points

Workday and SAP work well, but they're built for enterprise scale and priced to match. Mid-size IT companies with 500 to 5,000 employees are in a gap where lightweight HRMS tools don't go deep enough, but full enterprise platforms are too heavy and too expensive. Nobody's built the right-sized version for IT delivery models at this segment.

The bigger issue is that the data is scattered. HR lives in Greythr or Darwinbox. Allocation gets tracked in spreadsheets. Project capacity lives in Jira or Notion. None of these talk to each other, so answering a straightforward question like "who can start a project next week?" takes a sync call and a manual export. I ran 6 interviews with HR managers, engineering leads, and project coordinators. The same things came up every time: bench visibility was blind, weekly utilisation reports were built manually on Friday afternoons, and leave and project data lived in completely different places. Nobody had one screen that answered leadership's questions.

Design Decisions

Fixed sidebar, not top nav. Power users move between Employees, Allocation, and Dashboard constantly. A fixed sidebar with an active-state left border keeps every module one click away and makes it obvious where you are at all times.

Monospace numerals across all data. Proportional fonts make number columns shift as values change in digit count. Using Inter for numerical data keeps columns aligned and gives the product a proper data-tool feel.

Inline filters, not a filter drawer. HR managers filter repeatedly throughout a session. A drawer hides the table state while you're filtering, which adds cognitive load. The filter bar sits above every table, always visible, so the current filter combination is readable at a glance.

Semantic colour borders on KPI cards. Early versions had icons inside the cards, which competed with the numbers. I replaced them with a 2px top border in semantic colours: blue, green, amber, red. Urgency reads instantly without adding visual noise.

Dark mode as a real feature, not an afterthought. 4 of 6 people in research mentioned it as a deal-breaker. Dark mode was built with 40+ scoped CSS overrides, every component tested in both themes, persisted to localStorage.

Modal-based CRUD to preserve context. Full-page navigation for something like "Add Employee" wipes scroll position, filters, and the list the user was reviewing. All create and edit actions open as overlays. Esc always closes. Context stays intact unless the user explicitly leaves.

This flow starts from the Employees section, where selecting a row opens a detailed profile panel with key information. From there the user can assign the employee to a project, seamlessly transitioning to the Allocation page with the user pre-selected and highlighted. The process concludes with reviewing the timeline and saving the allocation, supported by clear confirmation feedback.

Creating a new project in WorkGrid used to mean filling out a single overwhelming form. We broke it into three intentional steps - define the project, build the team, set the timeline giving managers clarity at every stage and confidence before they hit create.

My Contribution

I ran 6 user interviews to understand the pain points, then built a primary persona around Ravi Kumar, a Senior HR Manager at an IT services company in Bangalore who opens 3 spreadsheets every morning before his actual work starts and builds the CTO's weekly headcount summary in PowerPoint. His core need was simple: any availability question answered in under 30 seconds.

From there I designed the full information architecture across 7 modules, mapped the primary user flow around the most-used task (allocating an employee to a project), and built wireframes before moving into high-fidelity in Figma. The product was then co-built with Claude AI as an engineering partner and deployed to live production. The whole cycle research to deploy took 10 weeks, solo.

Output & Learnings

The product shipped with 7 functional modules, 12k+ employee records, 8,032 timesheet entries, and 1,314 leave records all connected through a single data model. Three SaaS pricing tiers, full dark mode, zero full-page navigations for any CRUD action.

Building it solo pushed me to think like a product team, not just a designer. The decisions I got right early modals over page navigation, CSS variables from the start, realistic data for testing saved significant time later. What I'd do differently: Figma prototyping before jumping to code would have caught interaction issues earlier. The allocation timeline still needs more testing. And there's no onboarding flow, which matters more than I initially thought for a product this dense. Those are the things I'd fix in a v2.
